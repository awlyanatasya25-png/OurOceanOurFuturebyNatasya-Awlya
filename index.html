The Environmental Health Issue

Marine Plastic Pollution:
A Health & Education Emergency

Indonesia sits at the heart of the Coral Triangle — the most biodiverse marine region on Earth. Yet it also ranks among the world's top contributors to ocean plastic waste, threatening marine ecosystems, food security, and human health.

Microplastics now enter the human food chain through contaminated fish and seafood. Studies have detected plastic particles in the blood, lungs, and placentas of Indonesians. Coastal communities who depend on the sea for their livelihoods face shrinking catches and rising illness.

This is not just an environmental crisis — it is a public health emergency rooted in a systemic failure of environmental education. Neither the full scope of ocean health, nor the human dimensions of plastic pollution, are meaningfully addressed in Indonesia's national curriculum (Kurikulum Merdeka).

"The essence of Ocean Literacy is understanding the ocean's influence on us and our influence on the ocean."

— IOC-UNESCO, A New Blue Curriculum (2022)

Understanding the Crisis

Marine Plastic Pollution:
Causes, Impacts & Urgency

From river systems to dinner plates, plastic pollution follows a devastating chain that connects human behaviour, environmental degradation, and public health outcomes.

🋰

The Source

75%

Of Indonesia's plastic waste enters waterways through improper disposal, inadequate waste management infrastructure, and a culture of single-use plastics. Major rivers like the Citarum — dubbed the world's most polluted river — carry millions of tonnes to the sea annually.

🐞

The Ecosystem Impact

50%

Of Indonesia's coral reefs are moderately to severely degraded, partly due to plastic entanglement, smothering, and chemical leaching. Coral reefs support 25% of all marine life and protect 60 million Indonesians living in coastal zones from storm surges.

🥘

Human Health Risk

11M

Tonnes of plastic enter the global ocean annually. Microplastics — fragments under 5mm — are now found in Indonesian seafood, drinking water, and even in the blood and breast milk of coastal residents, linked to hormonal disruption, inflammation, and carcinogenic risk.

📈

Economic Damage

$13B

Annual economic damage to marine and coastal tourism industries across Southeast Asia from plastic pollution (UNEP, 2016). Indonesian fishing communities report catch declines of 30–50% in heavily polluted coastal areas, deepening food insecurity and poverty.

🏥

The Climate Link

50%

Of Earth's oxygen is produced by ocean phytoplankton. Plastic pollution suppresses phytoplankton growth, reducing the ocean's capacity to absorb CO₂ — the very climate regulation function that makes Earth habitable. Ocean health and climate crisis are inseparable.

📚

The Education Gap

Low

Indonesia's Kurikulum Merdeka introduces environmental themes but lacks systematic ocean literacy, plastic pollution health impacts, or action-oriented competencies. Without curriculum transformation, knowledge gaps perpetuate behaviours that drive pollution.

Indonesia's Plastic Crisis: A National Emergency

Indonesia produces approximately 6.8 million tonnes of plastic waste annually, and over 620,000 tonnes enter its surrounding seas. The country's 17,000 islands, 99,000 km of coastline, and 270 million population create both enormous vulnerability and extraordinary responsibility.

The Indonesian government's National Action Plan on Marine Debris (2017–2025) committed to reducing marine plastic pollution by 70% — but implementation has been inconsistent, underfunded, and hampered by low public awareness rooted in the education deficit.

Waste-pickers and fishing communities in Java, Sulawesi, and Kalimantan report respiratory problems, skin diseases, and gastrointestinal illness linked to plastic burning and microplastic ingestion — a public health emergency invisible in national health statistics.

99% Of seabirds will have ingested plastic by 2050 (CSIRO) Source: Commonwealth Scientific & Industrial Research Organisation

Biodiversity Collapse at the Coral Triangle

Indonesia lies at the heart of the Coral Triangle — a region covering just 1.6% of ocean surface but containing 76% of the world's coral species, 37% of reef fish species, and critical nesting grounds for six of the world's seven sea turtle species.

Plastic pollution, combined with rising sea temperatures from climate change, is driving coral bleaching events of increasing severity. The 2016 and 2019 bleaching events damaged over 50% of Indonesian reefs — ecosystems that protect coastal communities, sustain fisheries, and generate tourism revenue worth $3.1 billion annually.

For Indonesian students, this is not an abstract global problem — it is an unfolding catastrophe in their own waters, affecting their families, food, and futures. Yet the curriculum rarely makes this connection explicit.

Curriculum Innovation

Blue & Green Curriculum:
The Educational Response

Two transformative UNESCO frameworks offer a roadmap: the Green Curriculum for climate action, and the Blue Curriculum for ocean literacy. Together, they address both the causes and solutions to marine plastic pollution.

What is the Blue Curriculum?

Developed by IOC-UNESCO (2022), the Blue Curriculum is a framework for integrating Ocean Literacy into national education systems. It is built on the understanding that "the ocean's influence on us and our influence on the ocean" must be understood by all citizens.

The Blue Curriculum uses a Theory of Change model with four components: Knowledge & Awareness, Nature-Connectedness, Values & Attitudes, and Competences — to move learners from passive awareness to active ocean stewardship.

Its seven foundational principles assert that: the ocean makes Earth habitable; the ocean drives climate; the ocean supports all life; the ocean and humans are inextricably linked; and the ocean is largely unexplored. These principles should underpin all subjects — not just science.

"Ocean Literacy has grown into an approach for society as a whole that catalyzes actions to protect, conserve and sustainably use the ocean."

— IOC-UNESCO, A New Blue Curriculum (2022)

7 Principles of Ocean Literacy

1

One big ocean, many features

Earth's ocean is interconnected; local actions have global consequences.

2

Ocean shapes Earth's features

Coastlines, geology, and atmospheres are products of ocean processes.

3

Ocean influences climate

Ocean currents regulate temperature, weather, and carbon cycles globally.

4

Ocean makes Earth habitable

It produces oxygen, moderates temperature, and drives the water cycle.

5

Ocean supports all life

Biodiversity from coral reefs to deep-sea vents sustains planetary health.

6

Ocean and humans are linked

Culture, economy, food, medicine — all are profoundly shaped by the sea.

7

Ocean is largely unexplored

Over 80% remains unmapped — inspiring curiosity and scientific careers.

What is the Green Curriculum (Greening)?

UNESCO's Greening Curriculum Guidance (2024) aims to make all learners climate-ready through action-oriented, holistic, scientifically accurate, and justice-driven education. It responds directly to youth demands for transformative climate learning.

The Greening framework covers six key concepts: Climate Science, Ecosystems & Biodiversity, Climate Justice, Resilience-Building, Post-Carbon Economies, and Sustainable Lifestyles. Marine plastic pollution intersects all six.

Rather than treating climate as a single subject, Greening embeds it across all disciplines: geography, science, arts, social studies, health, and more — reflecting the cross-cutting nature of environmental health issues like ocean pollution.

"Prioritizing the development of learners' sense of efficacy towards climate action — not just knowledge — is essential for driving behaviour change."

— UNESCO, Greening Curriculum Guidance (2024)

Key Principles of Greening Education

Education must go beyond imparting knowledge. Research shows that knowledge alone does not drive behaviour change. Green curriculum builds learner agency, emotional engagement, and skills for real-world action — including addressing marine pollution in their own communities.
Learning is most effective when connected to local environments and lived experiences. For Indonesian students, this means learning about the Coral Triangle, local river systems, and the plastic crisis affecting their families' fishing livelihoods — not just abstract global statistics.
Marine plastic pollution is simultaneously a chemistry problem, a social justice issue, an economics challenge, a public health threat, and a cultural question. Green curriculum integrates these perspectives across subjects rather than siloing them in science class alone.
The poorest coastal communities suffer the most from pollution they contribute least to. Green curriculum frames environmental health as a justice issue — teaching students to recognise power imbalances, historical inequalities, and the disproportionate burden on marginalized groups.
Learning doesn't stop at the school gate. Greening education extends into communities through partnerships with NGOs, local governments, traditional knowledge holders, and fishing communities — creating a connected ecosystem of environmental learning.

Why Blue + Green Together?

Marine plastic pollution is a nexus issue that sits precisely at the intersection of ocean health (Blue) and climate action (Green). Neither framework alone is sufficient — but together, they offer a comprehensive educational response to one of Indonesia's most urgent environmental crises.

Blue Curriculum contributes

Ocean as Living System

Students learn the 7 principles of Ocean Literacy — understanding how plastic disrupts marine food webs, kills coral reefs, and enters human bodies through the food chain.

Green Curriculum contributes

Climate & Health Justice

Students understand plastic production's carbon footprint, its disproportionate health impact on poor coastal communities, and the systemic economic changes needed for a post-plastic economy.

Blue Curriculum contributes

Nature-Connectedness

Field trips to mangroves, coral snorkelling, citizen science beach surveys — building emotional connection to the marine environment as the foundation for stewardship behaviour.

Green Curriculum contributes

Action & Competence

Students develop skills in advocacy, community organising, sustainable consumption, and innovation — from designing plastic alternatives to campaigning for waste management policy reform.

Together, Blue + Green Curriculum creates ocean-literate, climate-ready, justice-conscious learners who can act.

Indonesia in Focus

The Indonesian Context:
Opportunity at the Coral Triangle

Indonesia is simultaneously the world's largest archipelago, a major ocean plastic polluter, the heart of global marine biodiversity, and a rapidly growing educational system reaching 68 million students. No country has more at stake — or more potential — in ocean education reform.

Current State of the Kurikulum Merdeka

Launched in 2022, Kurikulum Merdeka (Freedom Curriculum) represents a genuine attempt at progressive education reform in Indonesia — emphasising student-centred learning, project-based approaches (Projek Penguatan Profil Pelajar Pancasila / P5), and cross-disciplinary integration.

The P5 framework includes environmental sustainability as one of six character profiles, and teachers have some flexibility to design projects around local environmental issues. This is a genuine foothold for Blue and Green curriculum integration.

✗ What's Missing

  • No systematic Ocean Literacy framework or ocean-specific learning outcomes
  • Marine plastic pollution not featured as a health issue in science standards
  • Climate justice and equity dimensions largely absent
  • Coastal and marine local wisdom (kearifan lokal) rarely integrated
  • Action-oriented competencies for environmental change underemphasised
  • No mandatory assessment of environmental health knowledge

✓ Existing Footholds

  • P5 (Profil Pelajar Pancasila) includes environmental themes with flexibility
  • Project-based learning model is compatible with place-based ocean education
  • Science curriculum covers ecosystems and pollution at basic level
  • Strong community (gotong royong) values align with collective environmental action
  • Pesantren (Islamic boarding schools) and local schools already do environmental projects
  • Government has marine spatial planning commitments aligned to SDG 14

"Indonesia's Kurikulum Merdeka creates openings for curriculum innovation — but without explicit Blue and Green frameworks, environmental health education remains fragmented, shallow, and disconnected from student lives."

— Campaign analysis based on UNESCO frameworks (2022, 2024)

The Global-Local Link

Globally, UNESCO's Greening Education Partnership (GEP) has set a target of 90% of countries integrating climate change in curricula by 2030. Indonesia has not yet developed a comprehensive Ocean Literacy framework aligned with the Blue Curriculum, nor a systematic greening plan for its national curriculum. In comparison, countries like Portugal, Brazil, and Kenya are already piloting Blue Curriculum approaches (IOC-UNESCO, 2022).

At the local level, communities in Wakatobi, Raja Ampat, and the Banda Sea are witnessing the direct impacts of marine plastic pollution on their traditional fishing practices, sacred marine areas, and tourism economies. Their knowledge — and their urgency — should be at the centre of a reformed curriculum.

Implementation Strategies

Practical Steps for
Educational Transformation

Based on UNESCO's Blue Curriculum Toolkit and Greening Curriculum Guidance, here are concrete, realistic strategies for integrating ocean health education across Indonesian educational settings.

For Teachers

Transform Your Classroom Into a Blue-Green Learning Space

You don't need to overhaul the curriculum — start with your existing subjects and expand outward.

  • Use P5 projects (Projek Penguatan Profil Pelajar Pancasila) to design local plastic waste solutions — from beach audits to community campaigns
  • Integrate ocean data into maths and science: graph microplastic concentrations, calculate marine debris accumulation rates, map local rivers to the sea
  • Teach plastic pollution as a health issue in biology: trace microplastics through the food chain to human bodies — making it viscerally relevant
  • Use local literature and oral traditions (sastra laut, pantun) to teach values of ocean stewardship through Bahasa Indonesia and arts lessons
  • Connect with Marine Protected Area rangers, fishing cooperatives (KUD Nelayan), and NGOs like WWF Indonesia for guest speakers and field partnerships
  • Access IOC-UNESCO's Ocean Literacy Portal (oceanliteracy.unesco.org) for free, teacher-ready lesson resources adaptable to Indonesian contexts
  • Facilitate climate anxiety circles (as recommended by UNESCO GEP, 2024): creating safe spaces for students to process ocean grief and build resilience
For Students

Become an Ocean Advocate and Citizen Scientist

Your generation has the most at stake — and the most power to drive change, starting now.

  • Join or start a Trash Hero or Pandu Laut Nusantara beach/river clean-up club at your school — turning action into learning
  • Conduct a plastic audit of your school canteen: identify single-use plastics, track sources, propose alternatives, and present findings to the school committee
  • Participate in citizen science programs: the Marine Debris Tracker app allows students to record and map plastic pollution data contributing to global research
  • Create documentary short films or social media content about local fishing communities impacted by plastic pollution — giving voice to those most affected
  • Learn about traditional Bajo sea nomad practices and other Indonesian maritime cultures for lessons in sustainable ocean relationships
  • Organise a plastic-free school challenge and advocate to the principal and local government for a school plastic ban policy
  • Connect with Youth for Ocean Indonesia and similar networks to amplify your voice at provincial and national policy levels
For Communities & Policymakers

Build the Infrastructure for Ocean Education

Sustainable curriculum change requires systemic support beyond the classroom.

  • Advocate to Kemendikbudristek (Ministry of Education) to develop an explicit Ocean Literacy framework aligned with UNESCO's Blue Curriculum Toolkit as a supplement to Kurikulum Merdeka
  • Fund teacher training programs in environmental health education — partnering with LIPI (Indonesian Institute of Sciences) and university marine departments
  • Establish school-Marine Protected Area partnerships in coastal provinces, making MPAs living classrooms for ocean education (following the Portuguese model in UNESCO's Blue Curriculum case studies)
  • Integrate kearifan lokal (local ecological knowledge) from Bajo, Bugis, and Batak maritime traditions into curriculum materials — decolonising ocean education
  • Create mangrove and reef restoration projects as mandatory P5 components in coastal school districts, connecting restoration science with community livelihoods
  • Fund community waste banks (bank sampah) that partner with schools, turning circular economy concepts into practised reality
  • Develop Indonesia-specific Ocean Literacy assessment tools, working with PUSPENDIK (Education Assessment Centre) to track progress toward UNESCO's 2030 targets

A Phased Implementation Roadmap for Indonesia

Based on UNESCO's 10-step Greening Curriculum implementation model, adapted for the Indonesian context.

Phase 1 · 2025–2026

Foundation

Map existing curriculum footholds. Commission Indonesia-specific Ocean Literacy report. Pilot Blue-Green P5 projects in 50 coastal schools across 5 provinces.

Phase 2 · 2026–2028

Scale

Develop national Ocean Literacy learning outcomes framework. Train 10,000 teachers in 14 coastal provinces. Publish Indonesia-specific Blue-Green curriculum resources in Bahasa Indonesia.

Phase 3 · 2028–2030

Mainstream

Integrate Ocean Literacy into national curriculum standards. Launch assessment framework. Achieve UNESCO GEP target: Indonesia's curriculum meets 90% ocean-climate integration benchmark by 2030.

I’m Natasya Awlya, a student in the International Program on Science Education at Universitas Pendidikan Indonesia (UPI), with a strong interest in environmental health and education for sustainability.

This website is part of a campaign to raise awareness about marine plastic pollution as an environmental and public health issue, especially in the Indonesian context. As a country surrounded by oceans, Indonesia faces complex challenges that are often overlooked in everyday learning.

Through this platform, I aim to bridge the gap between scientific knowledge and real-life issues by presenting content in a way that is clear, structured, and meaningful. The goal is not only to inform, but also to encourage deeper understanding and critical thinking.

This project also highlights the importance of integrating Blue Curriculum and Green (Greening) Curriculum into education, so that students are not only aware of environmental problems, but are also equipped to respond to them.

Let’s learn, reflect, and take action together for a healthier ocean and a more sustainable future.
